About the Collaboration
Artist Bio

There is a reason I never make books as part of my art production. It is a medium I find outside of my conceptual interest. Don’t get me wrong— I love well-made and beautiful artists’ books. I just simply never have had an interest in making one myself. So, when Britt asked me to participate in this project I said yes to the challenge, knowing it was going to be difficult. If I was honest, in my deepest desires, I wanted red velveteen just once in my career and maybe this was the moment. But I was mistaken. I soon realized that Britt wanted to get beyond the romanticizing of book production and prove they could be and are much more, and it was my own voice as a conceptually oriented artist that could help emphasize the point. For the last several years I have been collecting Mt. Rushmore souvenirs. I collect them because, as a trained sculptor, I have a curiosity with monuments in general. But with this particular monument I have a deep fascination. There are many reasons for this interest; it’s scale, it’s absurdness, it’s iconic value and simply it’s shear physical attraction. My goal was to de-monumentalize this monument— hence the title Book of Many Faces came into being. Then, in thinking about the combination of the notion of the book as a democratic object and the historic idea of Mount Rushmore, the subtitle “Shrine of Democracy” presented itself. Indeed isn’t that why so many presidential candidates get their photo taken in front of it? (Wishful thinking perhaps?) I also noticed that almost every souvenir I have collected had an interpretation of some sort with each president’s face. Some didn’t even bear any resemblance to the president depicted. The souvenirs, were, in essence, all people embedded in this monument, they were tributes to the proverbial everyman. That, along with my newly purchased vintage Mt. Rushmore souvenir bookends, is what brought the conceptual framework for “ Book of Many Faces” together. I see the “Book of Many Faces” as a conceptual de-monumentalizing of Mt. Rushmore. The book itself, this object in its simplicity of form, is now the true “Shrine of Democracy”.

b. 1956
Campbelltown, PA
Mel Ziegler began his undergraduate studies at the Rhode Island School of Design, later transferring to the Kansas City Art Institute to complete his BFA in 1978. He earned an MFA from the California Institute of the Arts in Valencia in 1982. It was in Kansas City that he met Kate Ericson, his future artistic collaborator of 18 years. Together, Ericson and Ziegler made influential site-specific installations and objects concerned with mapping trajectories, questioning history, and highlighting the specificity of places and communities— all themes that had also been important for Ziegler in his early solo works. After the tragic and premature death of his partner Kate Ericson in 1995, Mel Ziegler has continued to show works nationally and internationally. Many of his early works are compiled in his exhibition catalogue of the same name stuffed (2003), for the Secession, Vienna, Austria; additional images of recent works can be viewed at www.melziegler.com. Ziegler earned a Loeb Fellowship for study at the Graduate School of Design at Harvard University in 1996-97. He continues to lecture and exhibit throughout the United States, Europe and South America. He recently completed several new projects, Downtown Mixer, in Houston Texas involving collecting breath samples from over 1600 participants in eight different high-rises, Messages from Murray in Murray, Kentucky involving the community writing messages on packing boxes from local manufacturers and most recently a project for Cheekwood Museum of Art and Botanical Garden called Smell The Flowers involving the collaboration of the US Military. He completed, in 2013, a major public art commission for the Art In Public Places Program, Cambridge, MA titled “ Taking Note” for the Rindge and Latin High School. He has recently received the Big Bang award from the University of Virginia in Charlottesville, VA. Ziegler spent ten years as a Professor of Sculpture at the University of Texas, Austin. He is in his seventh year as a Professor of Art and Chair of the recently created Department of Art at Vanderbilt University in Nashville , TN. He is currently working on series of agricultural projects by collaborating with farmers in various rural communities across the United States. These continuing projects have already taken place in rural Iowa, Illinois, Kansas, Nebraska, Missouri, Texas and South Dakota. Currently, in Nebraska he is creating the Sandhills Institute for Civic Engagement, Ecology and the Arts.
